  • Suspension Settings

The Wheels panel handles tire friction, wheel dimensions, and alignment. These settings fundamentally affect how the vehicle grips the road and corners.

Important: Suspension settings (spring, damper, travel distance) are configured in the Vehicle Customization panel, not the Wheels panel. See the "Suspension Settings" section below for details.

What This Panel Configures

Tire Friction

The grip characteristics of the tires:

Forward Friction

Grip along the direction of wheel rotation (acceleration/braking):

  • Stiffness: How quickly grip builds up
  • Extremum slip/value: Peak grip point
  • Asymptote slip/value: Sliding grip

Sideways Friction

Grip perpendicular to wheel rotation (cornering):

  • Same parameters as forward friction
  • Typically different values for realistic behavior

Grip Multipliers

Overall grip scaling factors for different conditions:

  • Dry grip multiplier
  • Wet grip multiplier
  • Surface-specific multipliers

Wheel Alignment

The geometry of how wheels are mounted:

Camber

Wheel tilt when viewed from front:

  • Negative camber: Top of wheel tilts inward
  • Improves cornering grip
  • Reduces straight-line grip

Caster

Steering axis tilt when viewed from side:

  • Affects steering feel and self-centering
  • Higher caster = more stable, heavier steering

Toe

Wheel angle when viewed from above:

  • Toe-in: Wheels point slightly inward
  • Toe-out: Wheels point slightly outward
  • Affects stability and turn-in response

Per-Axle Configuration

Settings can differ between front and rear axles:

  • Different friction for front vs rear balance
  • Different wheel widths (staggered setup)
  • Different alignment for specific behaviors

Common Configurations

High-Grip Track Setup 


Configure for maximum track grip:

- High sideways friction stiffness for quick response

- Forward friction optimized for late braking

- 2-3 degrees negative front camber

- Wide tires for maximum contact patch

- High grip multiplier (1.5+)

Drift Setup 


Set up for drifting:

- Slightly reduced rear sideways friction (about 85% of front)

- Lower rear grip multiplier (0.9)

- Slightly higher front grip for steering control (1.1)

- Negative front camber for grip during drift

- Wider rear tires for smoky slides

Comfortable Daily Driver 


Configure for comfort:

- Balanced friction front to rear

- Standard wheel width

- Minimal alignment adjustments (near zero)

- Normal grip multiplier (1.0)

Off-Road Configuration 


Set up for off-road:

- Reduced overall grip for realistic loose surfaces

- Conservative alignment for predictability

- Lower friction values for dirt/gravel

Understanding Friction Curves

The Friction Model

RCCP uses a friction curve with key points:

  1. Extremum Point: Peak grip before sliding begins
  • Slip value: How much wheel slip at peak
  • Force value: Maximum grip force available
  1. Asymptote Point: Sustained sliding grip
  • Slip value: Full sliding slip amount
  • Force value: Grip while sliding

Stiffness

How quickly grip builds as slip increases:

  • High stiffness: Grip comes on suddenly (responsive)
  • Low stiffness: Grip builds gradually (forgiving)

Balancing Front vs Rear

More rear grip than front:

  • Understeer tendency
  • Stable, safe feeling
  • Good for novice-friendly vehicles

More front grip than rear:

  • Oversteer tendency
  • Rear steps out in corners
  • Good for drift or sporty feel

Balanced grip:

  • Neutral handling
  • Professional driving feel
  • Requires skill to maximize

Alignment Explained

Camber

Negative Camber (common):

  • Tops of wheels lean inward
  • Optimizes tire contact patch in corners
  • Reduces straight-line grip slightly
  • Race cars: 2-4 degrees
  • Street cars: 0.5-1.5 degrees

Zero Camber:

  • Wheels perfectly vertical
  • Maximum straight-line grip
  • Reduced cornering grip

Positive Camber (rare):

  • Tops of wheels lean outward
  • Poor grip characteristics
  • Generally not used

Caster

More Caster:

  • Stronger self-centering
  • More stable at speed
  • Heavier steering feel
  • Better straight-line stability

Less Caster:

  • Lighter steering
  • Less self-centering
  • Quicker turn-in

Toe

Toe-In (front wheels point inward):

  • Straight-line stability
  • Reduced turn-in response
  • Common for rear axle

Toe-Out (front wheels point outward):

  • Quicker turn-in
  • Less stable on straights
  • Common for front axle in race setups

Zero Toe:

  • Neutral handling
  • Default starting point

Tips for This Panel

Understand the Baseline

Before changing values, understand what the current setup does. Test drive first.

Change One Thing at a Time

Grip and alignment interact. Changing everything at once makes it hard to identify effects.

Match Front and Rear

Unless you want specific handling bias, keep front and rear grip characteristics related.

Consider the Use Case

Track setups differ wildly from comfort setups. Know your goal.

Test Extensively

Tire settings affect handling significantly. Test in various scenarios: acceleration, braking, cornering.

Suspension Settings

Suspension tuning is handled in the Vehicle Customization panel, not the Wheels panel. This separation exists because:

In Wheels Panel:

  • Tire friction and grip
  • Wheel alignment (camber, caster, toe)
  • Wheel dimensions and sizing

In Vehicle Customization Panel:

  • Spring stiffness (how firm the ride)
  • Damper rates (how quickly suspension settles)
  • Suspension travel distance
  • Anti-roll bar effectiveness

To tune suspension:

  1. Select your vehicle
  2. Switch to the Vehicle Customization panel
  3. Describe the suspension changes you want

Example suspension prompts for Customization panel: 


Stiffen the suspension for better cornering with less body roll.



Soften the suspension for a comfortable ride quality.

Set spring rate to 35000 and damper to 3500.



Increase suspension travel for off-road capability.

This separation allows the Wheels panel to focus on tire behavior while the Customization panel handles chassis dynamics.
