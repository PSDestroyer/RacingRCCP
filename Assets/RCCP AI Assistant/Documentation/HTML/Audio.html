The Audio panel configures engine sound for RCCP vehicles. The engine sound system uses multiple audio layers that blend based on RPM and load to create realistic engine audio.

What This Panel Configures

Engine Sound System Overview

RCCP's engine audio consists of multiple sound layers:

Idle Layer

The engine sound at rest or very low RPM:

  • Plays when vehicle is stationary or coasting
  • Typically a low, steady rumble
  • Volume fades as RPM increases

Low RPM Layer

Engine sound at low to mid RPM range:

  • Active during normal driving
  • Blends with idle at low end
  • Blends with mid layer at high end

Mid RPM Layer

Engine sound at medium RPM range:

  • The "meat" of the engine sound
  • Most commonly heard during acceleration
  • Carries the engine's character

High RPM Layer

Engine sound at high RPM range:

  • Screaming engine sound near redline
  • Dramatic, exciting quality
  • Blends out as RPM drops

Off Throttle Layer (optional)

Engine sound during deceleration:

  • Overrun, popping, burbling
  • Adds realism to coasting

Layer Configuration

Each layer has these properties:

RPM Range

When this layer is audible:

  • Minimum RPM: Where layer starts
  • Maximum RPM: Where layer ends
  • Overlap creates smooth transitions

Volume Curve

How loud the layer is across its RPM range:

  • Usually fades in and out at edges
  • Peak volume in the middle of range

Pitch Curve

How the sound's pitch changes with RPM:

  • Higher RPM = higher pitch
  • Curve shape affects perceived engine character

Audio Clips

The actual sound files used for each layer:

  • WAV or OGG format recommended
  • Should be recorded at consistent pitch
  • Loop-friendly for seamless playback

Common Configurations

Sports Car Engine 


Configure a high-revving sports car engine sound:

- Smooth idle with moderate volume

- Low layer up to 3000 RPM with warm tone

- Mid layer from 2500-5500 RPM carrying main character

- High layer from 5000-8000 RPM with exciting scream

- Emphasize high-RPM drama

Muscle Car Engine 


Set up an American muscle car sound:

- Aggressive, lopey idle

- Strong low-end rumble in low layer

- Powerful mid-range growl

- High layer less prominent (lower-revving engine)

- Overall deeper, more bass-heavy character

Economy Car Engine 


Configure a basic economy car engine:

- Quiet, unobtrusive idle

- Soft low and mid layers

- High layer relatively quiet (not meant to be exciting)

- Overall modest, refined character

Electric Vehicle 


Set up electric motor sound:

- No idle layer (silent at rest)

- High-pitched whine increasing with speed

- Single layer covering full "RPM" range

- Futuristic, sci-fi character

Understanding RPM Ranges

Layer Overlap

Layers should overlap for smooth transitions: 


Idle:  0 ------[=======]

Low:   1000 ------[=============]

Mid:   2500 ------------[=================]

High:  5000 --------------------[===========] 8000

Without overlap, transitions sound jarring.

Volume Curves

Shape Importance

Volume curves control when each layer is prominent:

Fade-In:

At the start of the RPM range, volume increases gradually.

Peak:

In the middle of the range, volume is at maximum.

Fade-Out:

At the end of the range, volume decreases gradually.

Describing Curves to AI 


The mid layer should fade in starting at 2500 RPM, reach full

volume by 4000 RPM, stay full until 5500 RPM, then fade out

by 6500 RPM.

Cross-Fade Between Layers

Where layers overlap, their volumes should cross-fade:

  • As low layer fades out, mid layer fades in
  • Total volume stays consistent
  • No sudden jumps or dips

Pitch Curves

Basic Concept

Pitch increases with RPM to simulate engine speed:

  • At 1000 RPM, pitch might be 0.5x
  • At 4000 RPM, pitch might be 1.0x
  • At 7000 RPM, pitch might be 1.5x

Linear vs Non-Linear

Linear Pitch:

Consistent pitch increase per RPM.

Simple and predictable.

Non-Linear Pitch:

Varies across RPM range.

More realistic for some engines.

Source Recording Pitch

The pitch curve should account for the source recording:

  • If recorded at 4000 RPM, that's your 1.0x reference
  • Lower RPM = lower pitch multiplier
  • Higher RPM = higher pitch multiplier

Enabling and Disabling Layers

Enabling Layers

By default, all available layers may be active. To enable a disabled layer: 


Enable the off-throttle layer for engine overrun sounds.

Set its volume to 60% of the on-throttle sound.

Disabling Layers

To disable layers you don't want: 


Disable the off-throttle layer for a simpler sound profile.

Or: 


Use only idle and mid layers for a basic two-layer setup.

Adding Custom Audio Clips

The AI can reference audio clips by name if they exist in your project: 


Use the "V8_Idle_Loop" clip for the idle layer.

Use "V8_Low_Accel" for the low layer.

Requirements for clips:

  • Must exist in the project
  • Should be loop-friendly (seamless endpoints)
  • Should be recorded at consistent pitch
  • WAV or OGG format for best quality

Prompt Examples

Aggressive Sound 


Make the engine sound more aggressive:

- Increase overall volume by 20%

- Add more bass to the low layer

- Make the high layer more prominent

- Extend the high layer to start earlier

Refined Sound 


Refine the engine sound for luxury feel:

- Reduce overall volume slightly

- Smooth out transitions between layers

- Make the high layer subtle

- Emphasize mid-range smoothness

RPM Range Adjustment 


The engine redlines at 7500 RPM now. Adjust the audio layers:

- Extend all layers proportionally

- High layer should peak around 7000 RPM

- Maintain smooth transitions

Fix Transition Issues 


There's a noticeable volume dip around 3500 RPM.

Increase the overlap between low and mid layers.

The transition should be smooth with no dips.

Tips for This Panel

Listen During Configuration

Play the vehicle in the editor while adjusting to hear changes immediately.

Match Audio to Engine Type

A V8 should sound different from an I4. Choose appropriate clips and settings.

Test Across RPM Range

Rev the engine through its full range to check all transitions.

Consider Gameplay

Some games want dramatic sound; others want realism. Tune for your goal.

Start with Defaults

RCCP includes default audio settings. Modify from those rather than starting from scratch.

Use Quality Source Audio

The best configuration won't fix poor quality audio clips. Start with good recordings.
